\*\*Step 3: Use the Right Potting Soil\*\*

While you can keep them in the current soil you bought them with, you can prevent pests and rotting by repotting them within a few weeks. 3. Repotting: I would allow the plants to remain in their current pots and only repot them when you see a good amount of roots crawling out of the drainage holes in the bottom of the pot. 
Stores don't usually invest in the best soil. With most houseplants, the best soil is the one that drains well. It's very simple, here's my recipe to avoid rot, pests, and water retention:\


* 50% succulent/cactus soil
*  50% perlite mixture. 

These are good and cheap options that you can find in any Home Depot, Lowe's, or Walmart nursery:

\- Miracle Gro Perlite

\- Miracle Gro Cactus, Palm, and Citrus Soil

Step 4: Don‚Äôt Overwater

Watering was initially tricky for me, but I learned to water only when the soil was dry 2 inches below the surface to prevent root rot. 

1\. Watering: Too much water can rot out the roots, so it's best to let the soil dry out between waterings. Bottom watering is my preferred method.


For Pothos, a good shcedule is every 10 days. A moisture meter helped take the guesswork out of watering. Trusting myself and adjusting as I observed my plants made all the difference. \[Get a moisture meter here]\([https://www.example.com/moisture-meter](https://www.example.com/moisture-meter)).
